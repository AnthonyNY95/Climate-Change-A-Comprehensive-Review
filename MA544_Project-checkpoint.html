Climate Change: A Comprehensive Review

By: Thomas Cruz, NDumo Smith, Papia Rahman

Abstract

Through a thorough analysis of Climate Change data collected by WorldBank, this paper aims at extracting meaningful climate information from noisy data in a more robust and reliable manner than before. In this paper we will present an initial ANOVA analysis to determine statistically influential factors on climate change in the United States of America. We will also describe and visually represent the data with graphs using different methods of clustering and regression (Correlation, PCA, Linear regression, k-means clustering, spectral clustering, agglomerative clustering, birch clustering, mean shift clustering, and optics clustering). Finally, this paper will discuss the results from each type of analysis.

Introduction¶

Climate change, once a controversial topic, is now considered a fact to most scientists and governments around the world. Climate change refers to the increase or decrease in temperature throughout different regions of the Earth as time passes. The data used for this review was obtained through the WorldBank and contained data on different climate change factors from the years 1990 - 2010. While the data set contains useful data on countries around the world this paper will primarily focus on the United States of America (USA) as other countries are missing some data entries. This paper aims at extracting meaningful climate information from noisy data in a more robust and reliable manner than before. In no way does this paper attempt to provide a solution for climate change, but rather, analyze and represent the data in more digestible ways for the reader.

Data Cleaning¶

The initial size of our data set was 13,512 x 28 and contained a lot of information that could not be used in this paper due to missing cells or irrelevance. We cleaned the data by converting all .. cells to null cells then removing any rows containing null cells since all the data was not present. We then dropped several columns such as Country Name, Series name, Scale, Decimals, and 2011. These columns were either redundant or did not have useful information. The next step we took was to filter the data relating to the USA since that is the country we will be focusing on during our experiments. We then took the transpose of the data set to have all the series codes as columns and corresponding years as rows. This will allow us to use the series codes as factors and the years 1990 2010 as the rows while conducting our experiments. The final step was to write a for loop that converted all of our columns from strings to float-64. This allows us to use each data entry as a numerical value rather than an object. The data set labeled definitions contains all the series codes, their names, and their definitions as a reference for this paper.

Normality¶

In order to validate our results from each experiment we must first confirm two assumptions. The first assumption is independence. This assumption means that all groups are mutually exclusive. This is validated through the definitions data set where we can see that each series code represents different data throughout the years.

The second assumption is Normality. We test for Normality in this section through the Shapiro-Wilk test: shapiro() method from scipy.stats. After running this test the result is presented as (W-test statistic, p-value).

The test is non-significant, W= 0.9444396495819092, p=0.2663561701774597, which indicates that the residuals are normally distributed. We also visually confirmed normality through a probability plot of the models residuals.
